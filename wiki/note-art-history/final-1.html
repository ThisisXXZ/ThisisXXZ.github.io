期末复习大纲

ARTH1001 西方艺术史 test #2 复习大纲。

Judith and Her Maidservant

gender morality

Artemisia Gentileschi’s Judith and Her Maidservant interprets gender through a powerful vision of female agency and moral courage. The painting shows Judith and her maidservant immediately after the decapitation of Holofernes, and the dramatic tenebrism emphasizes their determination rather than vulnerability. Judith’s firm posture, controlled expression, and the sword still in her hand overturn conventional expectations of passive femininity in seventeenth-century society. Historically, Gentileschi was one of the few prominent female artists of the Baroque era, and her personal experience with male violence has often led scholars to view this work as a statement against patriarchal power. The candlelight illuminating Judith’s face symbolizes the moral righteousness of her act, while the severed head sinking into shadow represents the defeat of tyranny and evil. The close physical cooperation between the two women further highlights a sense of female solidarity. Through these visual and historical elements, the painting combines Baroque realism with a striking commentary on gender, morality, and the justice of heroic female action.

Ecstasy of Saint Teresa

morality theatrical realism

This sculpture conveys a powerful vision of spiritual and moral experience, reflecting the emotional intensity encouraged by the Counter-Reformation. Bernini depicts the moment when an angel pierces Saint Teresa with a divine arrow, a scene she described in her own writings. The dramatic drapery, the angel’s soft smile, and Teresa’s backward-leaning body communicate a sense of overwhelming divine presence. Realistic carving of flesh and fabric makes the mystical experience feel physically tangible, consistent with Baroque aims to engage viewers emotionally. The hidden window above the sculpture floods the figures with natural light, turning the chapel into a theatrical stage and reinforcing the sensation of heavenly intervention. Historically, the Catholic Church promoted artworks like this to inspire devotion and affirm the truth of spiritual experience. By focusing on Teresa’s intense emotional reaction, the work also emphasizes individuality, presenting a deeply personal encounter with the divine. Overall, the sculpture uses theatrical realism and dramatic lighting to make a spiritual moment feel immediate and real.

Arrival of Marie de Medici at Marseilles

modernity gender

Rubens’s Arrival of Marie de’ Medici at Marseilles presents an early modern form of political propaganda, using Baroque spectacle to legitimize Marie’s authority. Visually, the work places Marie at the exact center of the composition, illuminated by a strong golden light to elevate her status almost to that of a goddess. Mythological figures such as Neptune and his sea nymphs rise from the water to welcome her, suggesting that even nature and the gods support her arrival in France. The richly colored draperies, dramatic gestures, and dynamic diagonals reflect Baroque techniques of theatricality and emotional appeal. Historically, the painting belongs to a large cycle commissioned by Marie herself to justify her rule as a foreign-born queen and regent in early 17th-century France. By combining myth and state ceremony, Rubens constructs a gendered image of royal femininity that is powerful, honored, and divinely sanctioned. The work thus comments on modern political culture, using art as an instrument to shape public perceptions of monarchy and female authority.

Lucretia

morality gender

Rembrandt’s Lucretia presents a deeply emotional interpretation of gender and morality within a patriarchal society. Rather than showing the full Roman legend, Rembrandt focuses on Lucretia’s private suffering after being raped by Tarquin, a crime that forced her to commit suicide to preserve her family’s honor. Her pale face, trembling posture, and the stark contrast between the white satin gown and the blood on her chest visually express both her vulnerability and her moral anguish. The absence of any male figures shifts attention from heroic narrative to the inner world of a violated woman trapped by societal expectations. Her raised hand holding the dagger symbolizes agency, yet her expression reveals the tragedy of being compelled to uphold an oppressive moral code. Through the subdued lighting and intimate framing, Rembrandt creates a psychological realism that highlights her individuality rather than her mythic role. The painting ultimately critiques a culture where a woman’s virtue is valued more than her life.

Saying Grace

morality gender

Chardin’s Saying Grace presents an ideal of bourgeois morality in eighteenth-century France by depicting a quiet scene of family virtue. The painting shows a mother teaching her children to pray before eating, suggesting that moral discipline begins in the domestic sphere. Visually, Chardin uses soft lighting, muted colors, and simple furniture to create an atmosphere of sincerity and modesty. These realistic details emphasize that moral goodness is found not in aristocratic luxury but in everyday family life. Historically, this reflects Enlightenment ideas that valued education, order, and domestic virtue, especially within the rising middle class. The mother’s gentle posture also aligns with contemporary beliefs that women were responsible for the moral upbringing of children. Overall, the work uses calm realism and intimate domestic imagery to promote a model of ethical living.

Marriage à la Mode

morality modernity

William Hogarth’s Marriage à la Mode is a moralizing work that criticizes the corruption and immorality of eighteenth-century London society. The series depicts an arranged marriage between a bankrupt aristocrat and a merchant’s daughter, exposing how marriage has become a transaction of money and status. Hogarth uses detailed interior settings—the cluttered rooms, scattered objects, and theatrical poses—to visually communicate the couple’s moral decay. The exaggerated expressions and satirical symbols, such as broken furniture and unpaid bills, highlight their irresponsibility and selfish indulgence. Historically, the work belongs to the Enlightenment culture of moral critique, when artists used prints as mass media to influence public opinion. By creating affordable engravings, Hogarth brought moral lessons to a broad urban audience, reflecting the modern public sphere emerging in London. The series ultimately suggests that moral corruption destroys both individuals and society, making the work a powerful visual sermon on the dangers of vanity and greed.

The Swing

morality gender

Fragonard’s The Swing defines femininity and masculinity through the lens of 18th-century French aristocratic culture. The young woman on the swing becomes the central object of the male gaze, as the hidden lover looks up her dress while her husband remains unaware. The delicate pinks, soft lighting, and flowing textures sensually enhance her desirability, revealing how Rococo artists celebrated female beauty as an object of amusement. At the same time, the playful gesture of her kicked shoe and the Cupid statue urging secrecy suggest an illicit love affair. These visual cues reflect the moral looseness and pleasure-seeking behavior of the French upper class before the Revolution. The painting thus expresses gender dynamics rooted in male desire and courtly flirtation. Through its luxurious brushwork and intimate garden setting, Fragonard communicates a world in which sensual pleasure outweighs moral restraint.

The Marlborough Family

morality gender individuality

The Marlborough Family expresses eighteenth-century British ideals about gender, individuality, and family morality. Reynolds arranges the figures in a harmonious and dignified composition, presenting the women with soft gestures that emphasize feminine grace, while the men appear more upright and authoritative, reflecting patriarchal expectations of leadership. Each figure is carefully individualized through their posture and expression, consistent with Enlightenment ideas about personal character. The warm lighting and refined costumes convey the family’s moral respectability and social responsibility. The affectionate interaction among the children and adults further reinforces the notion of domestic virtue. As a Grand Manner portrait, the work idealizes the aristocratic household and communicates modern ideas about identity, stability, and public reputation. Through these visual strategies, Reynolds constructs a moral and social ideal of the eighteenth-century British family.

Los Caprichos

morality modernity

Out Hunting for Teeth

This print from Goya’s Los Caprichos series uses grotesque fantasy to criticize the moral corruption of late-18th-century Spanish society. The scene shows a witch stealing teeth from a corpse, a direct reference to popular superstitions that promised magical powers.
Goya deliberately exaggerates the figure’s twisted pose and the dark, theatrical lighting to expose the irrationality behind such beliefs. Although fantastical, the image reflects real social behaviors, revealing the persistence of ignorance in a supposedly enlightened age. It comments on “modernity” by contrasting Enlightenment reason with the continued dominance of superstition in Spain. The harsh shadows and expressive lines communicate Goya’s moral disgust toward these practices.
Through satire, Goya warns that a society guided by superstition rather than reason inevitably falls into moral decay.

The Sleep of Reason Produces Monsters

In this print from Los Caprichos, Goya uses a symbolic scene to comment on the failure of modernity in late-18th-century Spain. A man, often interpreted as Goya himself, sleeps at his desk while monstrous owls and bats rise behind him, representing superstition and irrationality.
The strong chiaroscuro and swirling forms create a nightmarish atmosphere that embodies the dangers of abandoning reason. Historically, the work reflects Enlightenment debates about rationality and the persistence of ignorance in Spanish society. Goya suggests that without reason, society cannot achieve true modernity and instead produces moral and political “monsters.” The image thus blends psychological reality with social critique. Through the print medium, Goya addressed a broader public, using satire to warn of the consequences of a society governed by superstition rather than rational thought.

Abbey in the Oak Forest

morality modernity

This painting presents a deeply spiritual interpretation of reality, characteristic of German Romanticism. Friedrich depicts a ruined Gothic abbey surrounded by dead oak trees, using the winter setting and the fading light to evoke themes of death, faith, and transcendence. The tiny procession of monks carrying a coffin introduces a moral and spiritual dimension, suggesting human humility before nature and eternity. Visually, the stark verticals of the trees and the broken arches guide the viewer’s gaze upward, reinforcing a longing for the divine. The overwhelming scale of nature compared to the small human figures expresses a psychological reality of loneliness and contemplation. Historically, the ruined abbey reflects early 19th-century anxieties about the decline of traditional religion after the Enlightenment and the upheavals of the Napoleonic wars. Through sublime natural imagery, Friedrich transforms the landscape into a meditation on mortality and spiritual endurance.

Death of Sardanapalus

gender morality

Delacroix’s Death of Sardanapalus presents an extreme vision of gendered violence and moral collapse within an imagined Oriental palace. The painting shows the Assyrian king calmly reclining on his bed while women around him are dragged, stabbed, and forced to die, emphasizing the absolute power of the male ruler over female bodies. Delacroix uses swirling diagonal lines, dramatic contrasts of light and shadow, and an intense red palette to convey chaos, terror, and overwhelming emotional intensity. These visual techniques highlight how the women are treated as disposable objects of the king’s pleasure and authority. Historically, this work reflects the Romantic fascination with irrational violence and the critique of tyrannical power, drawing from Byron’s poem about a decadent Eastern monarch. The king’s cold detachment embodies a complete breakdown of moral responsibility. Through these elements, the painting comments on the dangers of absolute authority and the gendered cruelty embedded in such systems of power.

The Stone Breakers

reality modernity

Gustave Courbet’s The Stone Breakers is a key example of nineteenth-century Realism because it represents the harsh physical reality of modern labor rather than heroic or idealized scenes. Courbet shows two anonymous workers dressed in worn, patched clothing, emphasizing their poverty and lower-class status. Their faces are either hidden or turned away from us, which suggests that they stand for an entire social group rather than individual personalities. The rough texture of Courbet’s brushwork, along with the heavy stones and simple tools, conveys the exhausting, repetitive nature of their work. Historically, France in the 1850s was undergoing rapid industrialization and rising class inequality, and Realist artists argued that modern art should depict the real conditions of ordinary people. By placing laborers on a large canvas usually reserved for history painting, Courbet challenged academic traditions and confronted viewers with uncomfortable social truths. The painting therefore defines “reality” as the lived experience of working-class people and uses both composition and texture to express this new, modern vision of society.

Olympia

gender modernity

Manet’s Olympia presents a striking interpretation of gender and modernity in mid-19th-century Paris. The nude woman looks directly at the viewer with an assertive gaze, reversing the traditional passive role of female nudes in earlier academic and mythological painting. Her upright posture, the tense hand covering her genitals, and the presence of luxurious accessories all identify her as a contemporary courtesan rather than a timeless goddess. This challenges the gender norms of the male gaze by showing a woman who is aware of being looked at and who controls her own visibility. The Black maid and the bouquet of flowers further imply a commercial exchange, exposing the reality of prostitution in modern urban life. Manet reinforces these ideas through flat lighting, abrupt outlines, and the rejection of classical idealization. Through these visual choices and its contemporary subject matter, Olympia comments on the shifting gender roles and the unsettling new realities of modernity in 1860s Paris.

Impression: Sunrise

reality modernity

Claude Monet’s Impression: Sunrise depicts a new kind of modern reality centered on the perception of light rather than detailed physical forms. The hazy blue-gray palette and the blurred outlines of the port show how early morning mist transforms the viewer’s visual experience. The bright orange sun and its reflection cut through the fog, emphasizing the fleeting atmospheric effects that Monet sought to capture in real time. These techniques reflect the Impressionist rejection of academic finish and the embrace of immediacy and outdoor observation. Historically, the work is set in the industrial port of Le Havre, whose cranes and smoke stacks appear faintly in the background, linking the painting to the modernizing world of nineteenth-century France. The emphasis on subjective appearance rather than objective description also highlights Monet’s individuality as an artist who foregrounds personal vision. Through its innovative brushwork and focus on sensory experience, the painting comments on the rapid, unstable, and modern nature of contemporary life.

The Cradle

gender individuality

Berthe Morisot’s The Cradle interprets femininity through the intimate space of motherhood.
The painting shows a young mother watching over her sleeping child, reflecting the 19th-century bourgeois ideal of maternal virtue. Morisot reinforces this idea through visual details such as the mother’s tilted posture, her gentle expression, and the translucent veil that creates a protective enclosure around the baby. The soft palette of pinks and whites emphasizes the domestic and feminine atmosphere.
Historically, women in 19th-century France were often confined to the private sphere, and Morisot—one of the few prominent female Impressionists—offers a distinctly female perspective on this space. At the same time, the loose brushwork and focus on a fleeting moment express Morisot’s own individuality and the Impressionist interest in personal vision.
Thus, the work combines gendered social meaning with a subjective, intimate expression of identity.

Nocturne in Black and Gold

modernity individuality

James Whistler’s Nocturne in Black and Gold comments on the modern experience of urban life by depicting a firework display in industrial London. Rather than presenting a clear narrative, Whistler focuses on the fleeting sensory impressions of the modern city—its smoke, artificial lights, and anonymous crowds. The falling golden sparks evoke the new forms of public entertainment that emerged in modern urban culture. Visually, the painting uses loose, scattered brushstrokes and an almost abstract composition to convey the atmosphere of a smoky night rather than detailed physical forms. The hazy silhouettes of figures emphasize the anonymity and collective nature of modern city crowds. Historically, Whistler advocated “art for art’s sake,” rejecting traditional storytelling and suggesting that modern art should capture sensations rather than moral lessons. His use of musical terminology (“nocturne”) further highlights the modern idea that painting can express mood and aesthetic experience. Through these techniques, the work presents a modern, subjective vision of reality shaped by the lights and instability of the modern world.

The Starry Night

realism individuality

Vincent van Gogh’s The Starry Night expresses a form of psychological reality rather than literal visual truth. The swirling sky, painted with thick impasto and rhythmic curves, conveys intense emotional energy that reflects the artist’s inner turmoil during his stay at the Saint-Rémy asylum. The dramatic contrast between the calm, geometric village and the turbulent, luminous sky highlights the tension between external order and internal chaos. Van Gogh’s use of unnatural colors—vivid yellows against deep blues—further emphasizes his highly personal interpretation of the night. These expressive techniques demonstrate the individuality central to Post-Impressionism, where artists sought to depict subjective experience instead of physical realism. The towering cypress, reaching upward like a flame, symbolizes spiritual longing and reinforces the painting’s emotional depth. Overall, the work communicates Van Gogh’s inner psychological landscape, redefining “reality” as the truth of personal feeling.

The Joy of Life

modernity individuality

Matisse’s The Joy of Life comments on the anxieties of modernity by creating a radically imaginative world that rejects naturalistic representation. Instead of depicting reality, he constructs an Arcadian landscape filled with naked figures who dance, rest, and play in complete harmony. Matisse uses intensely saturated, unnatural colors—pink trees, yellow fields, and orange bodies—to express emotional energy rather than observable fact. The flattened space and rhythmic curving lines dissolve any sense of realistic depth, emphasizing personal expression over objective depiction. This visionary landscape offers viewers an escape from the pressures of industrial modern society and proposes a more spiritual, joyful way of living. By rejecting academic technique and celebrating pure color, Matisse presents a modern visual language that reflects the individuality and emotional freedom of the artist.

Les Demoiselles d’Avignon

modernity individuality

Picasso’s Les Demoiselles d’Avignon comments on modernity and gender through a radical rejection of traditional realism. Instead of depicting the female nude as soft and idealized, Picasso fractures the bodies into sharp geometric forms, creating a conceptual rather than naturalistic reality. The confrontational gazes of the five women challenge the traditional male gaze and reflect the anxieties surrounding sexuality and prostitution in the modern city. Space is collapsed and unstable, with multiple viewpoints presented simultaneously, undermining the Renaissance system of linear perspective. Picasso was inspired by African masks and Iberian sculpture, incorporating non-Western forms to critique European artistic conventions. The violent lines, skewed anatomy, and aggressive composition express psychological tension rather than physical accuracy. By breaking every visual rule of his time, the painting embodies the cultural, artistic, and emotional rupture that defined early twentieth-century modernity. In this way, it uses innovative techniques to challenge norms of gender, desire, and visual representation.

Black Square

modernity reality

In Black Square, Malevich presents a radically modern interpretation of reality at a moment of dramatic cultural and political change in early twentieth-century Russia. Instead of depicting recognizable objects, he reduces the image to a single black square floating against a stark white background, eliminating perspective, light, and narrative. This extreme abstraction reflects his belief that modern art should no longer imitate the visible world but express the “pure feeling” of a new technological age. Exhibited in the 1915 “0.10” show and placed in the traditional “icon corner,” the work functioned as a kind of modern icon, symbolizing the overthrow of old artistic and spiritual systems. The contrast of geometric black and white creates an intense visual experience that communicates an inner, subjective reality rather than physical appearance. Through this non-objective form, Malevich asserts a new model of individuality, in which the artist’s personal vision replaces shared cultural representation. Overall, the painting comments on modernity by breaking decisively with five centuries of Western illusionism and proposing an entirely new artistic language for the modern world.

Fountain

modernity

Marcel Duchamp’s Fountain comments on the modern condition by rejecting traditional artistic values and embracing the industrial, technological world of the early twentieth century. Instead of depicting reality, Duchamp presents an actual mass-produced urinal, turning an ordinary object into art through the modern technique he called a “readymade.” Its factory-made porcelain surface and mechanical curves reflect the technological environment of World War I, while the signature “R. Mutt” mocks ideas of artistic authorship and craftsmanship. The work reflects the Dada movement’s belief that rationality and cultural institutions had failed, contributing to the violence and nationalism of the war. By placing this object in an exhibition context, Duchamp exposes the absurdity of artistic conventions and the instability of modern values. The piece therefore expresses a deeply modern skepticism about art, morality, and technology, using conceptual choice rather than hand-made skill to define artistic individuality.
